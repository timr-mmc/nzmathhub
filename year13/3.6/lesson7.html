Understanding Points of Inflection

A point of inflection is where a curve changes from being concave up to concave down (or vice versa). The curve changes its direction of bending.

Finding Points of Inflection

  1. Find $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$: Calculate the second derivative
  2. Set $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0$: Solve for $x$-values
  3. Test either side: Check if $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ changes sign
  4. Find $y$-coordinate: Substitute into original equation

⚠️ Important Distinction

Not all points where $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0$ are inflection points!

You MUST verify that the second derivative changes sign on either side of the point.

Horizontal Inflection

Both $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ AND $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0$

The curve is flat at the inflection point

Example: $y = x^3$ at $(0, 0)$

Non-Horizontal Inflection

$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0$ BUT $\frac{dy}{dx} \neq 0$

The curve has a gradient at the inflection point

Example: $y = x^3 + x$ at $(0, 0)$

Worked Examples

Example 1: Basic Cubic (Horizontal Inflection)

Question: Find the point of inflection of $y = x^3$
Step 1: First derivative ▼
$\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2$
Step 2: Second derivative ▼
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x$
Step 3: Solve $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0$ ▼
$6x = 0$, so $x = 0$
Step 4: Test sign change ▼
At $x = -0.1$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6(-0.1) = -0.6 < 0$ (concave down)
At $x = 0.1$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6(0.1) = 0.6 > 0$ (concave up)
Sign changes ✓
✓ Answer: Find $y$ ▼
$y = 0^3 = 0$
Point of inflection at $(0, 0)$

Example 2: Cubic with Linear Term (Non-Horizontal)

Question: Find inflection points of $y = x^3 + 3x$
Step 1: First derivative ▼
$\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 + 3$
Step 2: Second derivative ▼
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x$
Step 3: Solve ▼
$6x = 0$, so $x = 0$
Step 4: Verify sign change ▼
Changes from negative to positive ✓
✓ Answer ▼
At $x = 0$, $\frac{dy}{dx} = 3 \neq 0$
This is a non-horizontal inflection point
Point of inflection at $(0, 0)$ with gradient 3

Example 3: Not an Inflection Point!

Question: Find points of inflection of $y = x^4$
Step 1: Derivatives ▼
$\frac{dy}{dx} = 4x^3$, $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12x^2$
Step 2: Solve $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0$ ▼
$12x^2 = 0$, so $x = 0$
Step 3: Test sign change ▼
At $x = -0.1$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12(0.01) = 0.12 > 0$
At $x = 0.1$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12(0.01) = 0.12 > 0$
No sign change! ✗
✓ Conclusion ▼
NO point of inflection
$(0, 0)$ is actually a minimum point

Example 4: Multiple Inflection Points

Question: Find inflection points of $y = x^4 - 6x^2$
Step 1: Derivatives ▼
$\frac{dy}{dx} = 4x^3 - 12x$
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12x^2 - 12$
Step 2: Solve ▼
$12x^2 - 12 = 0$
$x^2 = 1$, so $x = 1$ or $x = -1$
Step 3: Test at $x = 1$ ▼
At $x = 0.5$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12(0.25) - 12 = -9 < 0$
At $x = 1.5$: $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12(2.25) - 12 = 15 > 0$
Sign changes ✓
Step 4: Test at $x = -1$ ▼
Similarly changes sign ✓
$y = 1 - 6 = -5$ at both points
✓ Answer ▼
Inflection points at $(1, -5)$ and $(-1, -5)$

Practice: Minimal Change

Set 1: Basic cubics with constant

Pattern: $y = x^3 + c$

a) Find point of inflection of $y = x^3$
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0$
Sign changes from negative to positive ✓
Point of inflection: $(0, 0)$
b) Find point of inflection of $y = x^3 + 2$
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0$
Sign changes ✓, $y = 2$
Point of inflection: $(0, 2)$
c) Find point of inflection of $y = x^3 - 5$
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0$
Sign changes ✓, $y = -5$
Point of inflection: $(0, -5)$

Set 2: Cubics with x² terms

Pattern: $y = x^3 - ax^2$

a) Find inflection point of $y = x^3 - 3x^2$
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x - 6 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 1$
Sign changes ✓, $y = 1 - 3 = -2$
Inflection at $(1, -2)$
b) Find inflection point of $y = x^3 - 6x^2$
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x - 12 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 2$
Sign changes ✓, $y = 8 - 24 = -16$
Inflection at $(2, -16)$
c) Find inflection point of $y = x^3 - 12x^2$
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x - 24 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 4$
Sign changes ✓, $y = 64 - 192 = -128$
Inflection at $(4, -128)$

Set 3: Testing for false inflections

Pattern: $y = x^4 + ax^2$ — be careful!

a) Find inflection points of $y = x^4$
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12x^2 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0$
Test: $x = -0.1$: positive, $x = 0.1$: positive
No sign change! Not an inflection point
b) Find inflection points of $y = x^4 + x^2$
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12x^2 + 2 > 0$ for all $x$
Never equals zero!
No inflection points
c) Find inflection points of $y = x^4 - 6x^2$
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12x^2 - 12 = 0 \Rightarrow x = \pm 1$
Both change sign ✓
Inflection points: $(1, -5)$ and $(-1, -5)$

Set 4: Complete analysis

a) Analyze $y = x^3 - 6x^2 + 9x + 1$ for inflection
$\frac{dy}{dx} = 3x^2 - 12x + 9$
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 6x - 12 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 2$
Sign changes ✓, $y = 8 - 24 + 18 + 1 = 3$
Non-horizontal inflection at $(2, 3)$
(Gradient at this point: $\frac{dy}{dx}|_{x=2} = 12 - 24 + 9 = -3$)
b) Analyze $y = 2x^3 - 3x^2 + 1$ for inflection
$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 12x - 6 = 0 \Rightarrow x = \frac{1}{2}$
Sign changes ✓
$y = 2(\frac{1}{8}) - 3(\frac{1}{4}) + 1 = \frac{1}{4} - \frac{3}{4} + 1 = \frac{1}{2}$
Inflection at $(\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2})$

Key Points to Remember

  • ✓ Points of inflection occur where $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0$ AND sign changes
  • ✓ Always test the sign change — don't assume!
  • ✓ If $\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$ too, it's a horizontal point of inflection
  • ✓ If $\frac{dy}{dx} \neq 0$, it's a non-horizontal point of inflection
  • ✓ Not all solutions to $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = 0$ are inflection points (e.g., $y = x^4$)
