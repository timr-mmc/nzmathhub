Understanding the Correlation Coefficient (r)

#
Definition: The correlation coefficient (r) is a numerical measure of the strength and direction of a linear relationship between two variables.

Key Properties of r

  • Range: $-1 \leq r \leq 1$
  • Unitless: r has no units (unlike the variables themselves)
  • Symmetric: swapping x and y doesn't change r
  • Measures only linear relationships

Interpreting r Values

r Value Interpretation
$r = 1$ Perfect positive linear relationship
$0.7 < r < 1$ Strong positive linear relationship
$0.3 < r < 0.7$ Moderate positive linear relationship
$0 < r < 0.3$ Weak positive linear relationship
$r = 0$ No linear relationship
$-0.3 < r < 0$ Weak negative linear relationship
$-0.7 < r < -0.3$ Moderate negative linear relationship
$-1 < r < -0.7$ Strong negative linear relationship
$r = -1$ Perfect negative linear relationship
💡 Remember: The sign tells us direction (+ or -), the magnitude tells us strength (closer to 1 or -1 means stronger).
2

Calculating the Correlation Coefficient

#

The Formula

$$r = \frac{n\sum xy - \sum x \sum y}{\sqrt{[n\sum x^2 - (\sum x)^2][n\sum y^2 - (\sum y)^2]}}$$

Where:

  • $n$ = number of data pairs
  • $\sum xy$ = sum of products of paired x and y values
  • $\sum x$ = sum of all x values
  • $\sum y$ = sum of all y values
  • $\sum x^2$ = sum of squared x values
  • $\sum y^2$ = sum of squared y values

Step-by-Step Example

Calculate r for this data:

x y $x^2$ $y^2$ $xy$
2 5 4 25 10
4 8 16 64 32
6 11 36 121 66
8 13 64 169 104
Σ = 20 Σ = 37 Σ = 120 Σ = 379 Σ = 212

Calculation:

$n = 4$, $\sum x = 20$, $\sum y = 37$, $\sum x^2 = 120$, $\sum y^2 = 379$, $\sum xy = 212$

$$r = \frac{4(212) - (20)(37)}{\sqrt{[4(120) - 20^2][4(379) - 37^2]}}$$ $$r = \frac{848 - 740}{\sqrt{[480 - 400][1516 - 1369]}}$$ $$r = \frac{108}{\sqrt{80 \times 147}}$$ $$r = \frac{108}{\sqrt{11760}}$$ $$r = \frac{108}{108.44} \approx 0.996$$

Interpretation: $r \approx 0.996$ indicates a very strong positive linear relationship.

💡 In Exams: You'll typically use a calculator's statistical functions to find r, but understanding the formula helps you interpret what r means.
3

Interpreting r in Context

#

Important Points

1. Correlation Does Not Imply Causation

A strong correlation doesn't mean one variable causes the other.

Example: Shark attacks and ice cream sales are correlated (both increase in summer), but one doesn't cause the other.

2. r Only Measures Linear Relationships

Non-linear relationships may have $r \approx 0$ even if a strong curved relationship exists.

3. Outliers Affect r

A single outlier can dramatically change the correlation coefficient.

4. Sample Size Matters

With small samples, r can be misleading. Larger samples give more reliable correlations.

Writing a Statistical Statement:

"There is a [strong/moderate/weak] [positive/negative] linear relationship between [x variable] and [y variable], with a correlation coefficient of $r =$ [value]. This suggests that as [x variable increases/decreases], [y variable] tends to [increase/decrease]."

Practice Example

For temperature (°C) and hot drink sales ($), we find $r = -0.85$.

Statistical Statement:

"There is a strong negative linear relationship between temperature and hot drink sales, with a correlation coefficient of $r = -0.85$. This suggests that as temperature increases, hot drink sales tend to decrease significantly."
