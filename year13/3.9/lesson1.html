What is Bivariate Data?

#
Definition: Bivariate data involves two variables measured on the same individual or object. We analyze how these variables relate to each other.

Key Concepts

Univariate Data: Data with one variable (e.g., heights of students)

Bivariate Data: Data with two variables (e.g., height AND weight of students)

Why Study Bivariate Data?

  • Understand relationships between variables
  • Make predictions based on known values
  • Identify patterns and trends in data
  • Detect unusual observations (outliers)
  • Make informed decisions using data

Variables in Bivariate Analysis

Explanatory Variable (Independent, x): The variable we think might influence the other

Response Variable (Dependent, y): The variable that might be affected by the explanatory variable

💡 Remember: Not all bivariate relationships are cause-and-effect. Correlation does not imply causation!
2

Types of Relationships

#

Positive Relationship

As one variable increases, the other variable also tends to increase.

Example: Study time and exam scores - more study usually leads to higher scores.

Negative Relationship

As one variable increases, the other variable tends to decrease.

Example: Speed and travel time - higher speed usually means less travel time.

No Relationship

The variables do not appear to have any consistent pattern.

Example: Shoe size and mathematical ability - typically no connection.

Strength of Relationship

  • Strong: Points cluster tightly around a pattern
  • Moderate: Points show a trend but with more scatter
  • Weak: Points show very little trend

Form of Relationship

  • Linear: Forms a straight-line pattern
  • Non-linear: Forms a curved pattern (not covered in Level 3)
3

Real-World Examples

#

Example 1: Temperature and Ice Cream Sales

Explanatory: Temperature (°C)

Response: Ice cream sales ($)

Expected Relationship: Positive - hotter days lead to more ice cream sales

Example 2: Car Age and Value

Explanatory: Age of car (years)

Response: Car value ($)

Expected Relationship: Negative - older cars typically worth less

Example 3: Advertising and Sales

Explanatory: Advertising spend ($)

Response: Product sales (units)

Expected Relationship: Positive - more advertising usually increases sales

Example 4: Height and IQ

Explanatory: Height (cm)

Response: IQ score

Expected Relationship: None - these variables are typically unrelated

📝 Activity: Think of 3 pairs of variables from your daily life. Identify which is explanatory and which is response, and predict the type of relationship.
