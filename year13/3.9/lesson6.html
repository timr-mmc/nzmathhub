Outliers Influential Other Features
1

What are Outliers?

#
Definition: An outlier is a data point that doesn't fit the general pattern of the relationship. It lies far from the line of best fit or from the cluster of other points.

Identifying Outliers

Look for points that:

  • Are far from the line of best fit (large residual)
  • Are separated from the main cluster of points
  • Don't follow the general trend
  • Have unusual x or y values compared to other data

Why Do Outliers Occur?

  1. Measurement Error: Incorrect recording or equipment malfunction
  2. Data Entry Error: Typing mistakes when entering data
  3. Genuine Unusual Cases: Legitimate but rare occurrences
  4. Different Population: Data from a different group mixed in

Example: Study Time vs Test Score

Most students show a positive relationship between study time and scores.

Outlier Example: A student who studied 10 hours but scored only 40%

Possible Explanations:

  • Student was unwell during the test
  • Studied the wrong material
  • Data recording error
  • Has learning difficulties not captured by study time alone
💡 Important: Never just delete outliers! Always investigate and explain them in context.
2

Influential Points

#
Definition: An influential point is one that, if removed, would significantly change the regression line (slope, intercept, or both).

Characteristics of Influential Points

  • Often have extreme x-values (far from other points horizontally)
  • Can be outliers, but not all outliers are influential
  • Have large effect on the correlation coefficient
  • Dramatically affect predictions

Effects of Influential Points

1. Increasing Correlation

An influential point aligned with the trend can make the correlation appear stronger than it really is.

Example: Most data shows weak correlation (r = 0.3), but one point far from others aligned with a positive trend increases r to 0.7.

2. Decreasing Correlation

An influential point not aligned with the trend can mask a real relationship.

Example: Strong positive trend (r = 0.9), but one outlier point reduces overall r to 0.5.

3. Changing the Slope

An influential point can make the line steeper or flatter.

What to Do About Influential Points

  1. Identify: Use scatter plots and calculate r with and without the point
  2. Investigate: Look for reasons why the point is unusual
  3. Report: Describe the point and its effect on the analysis
  4. Consider: Should it be kept or removed? Depends on context
📊 Best Practice: Report analyses both with and without influential points, explaining the difference in your interpretation.

Example Analysis Statement

"The scatter plot shows a strong positive relationship (r = 0.87) between advertising spend and sales. However, one point (spend = $50,000, sales = $200,000) is much higher than others and appears influential. Removing this point reduces the correlation to r = 0.62, suggesting a more moderate relationship for typical advertising levels. This point may represent a special campaign or different product and should be analyzed separately."
3

Other Unusual Features

#

1. Clusters

Definition: Distinct groups of points separated from each other.

Example: Height vs weight data showing two clusters - one for children, one for adults.

What to do: Consider analyzing clusters separately as they may represent different sub-populations with different relationships.

2. Gaps

Definition: Ranges of x-values where no data points exist.

Example: Car age vs value data missing ages 5-10 years.

Impact: Makes predictions in the gap range unreliable. The relationship might change in that range.

3. Non-linearity

Definition: The relationship curves rather than following a straight line.

Example: Year vs world record time shows diminishing returns (curved).

What to do: Linear regression is inappropriate for strongly curved relationships. At Level 3, acknowledge the non-linearity and discuss limitations.

4. Changing Variability

Definition: The scatter around the line increases or decreases across the x-range.

Example: Income vs savings - higher incomes show more variability in savings patterns.

Impact: Violates regression assumptions. Predictions may be less reliable, especially at ends of range.

Comprehensive Analysis Framework

When Describing Bivariate Data, Always Mention:
  1. Direction: Positive, negative, or no relationship
  2. Form: Linear or non-linear
  3. Strength: Strong, moderate, or weak (with r value)
  4. Unusual Features: Outliers, influential points, clusters, gaps
  5. Context: Explain findings in relation to the variables
  6. Limitations: Discuss any issues with the model or data

Example Student Answer

"The scatter plot shows a strong positive linear relationship between hours studied and test scores, with r = 0.85. This suggests that students who study more tend to achieve higher scores. However, there is one outlier: a student who studied 9 hours but scored only 45%. This point may represent a student who was unwell or studied ineffectively. Without this outlier, the correlation increases to r = 0.92, showing an even stronger relationship. The regression model explains 72% of the variation in test scores (R² = 0.72), indicating that study time is an important but not the only factor affecting test performance. Predictions are reliable within the observed range of 2-10 hours but should not be extrapolated beyond this range."
