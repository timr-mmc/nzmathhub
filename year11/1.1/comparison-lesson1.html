Writing Comparison Questions

#
Learning Objectives: Learn to write statistical investigative questions that compare two groups within a population. Master the three key components: two groups, a numerical variable, and a clear direction.

  • Identifying the three key components of comparison questions

  • Showing direction in your comparisons

📚 Core Concepts

What is a Comparison Question?

A comparison question is a statistical investigative question that compares two different groups within a population.

"Do dogs tend to be heavier than cats for pets of New Zealanders?"

This type of question helps us:

  • Understand differences between groups
  • Make informed decisions based on data

Bad Question vs Good Question

Let's see the difference!

❌ Bad Question:

"What pets do New Zealanders prefer?"

Problems:

  • No two groups being compared
  • "Prefer" is not numerical

✅ Good Question:

"Do dogs tend to be heavier than cats for pets of New Zealanders?"

Why it's better:

  • ✓ Two groups: dogs vs cats
  • ✓ Numerical: weight (kg)

The Three Key Components

Every good comparison question needs these three parts:

1. Two Groups
Dogs vs Cats
2. Numerical Variable
Weight (in kg)
3. Population
Pets of New Zealanders

Remember: The variable must be numerical (something you can measure), not categorical!

More Examples of Good Comparison Questions

"Do Year 9 students tend to spend more time on homework than Year 10 students at our school?"

Common Mistake #1

Not Actually Comparing Two Groups

❌ Bad: "What is the favourite sport at our school?"

Why is this bad?

This doesn't compare two groups - it's asking for a summary of everyone's preferences.

✓ Better: "Do Year 11 students tend to spend more hours per week playing sports than Year 12 students at our school?"

Common Mistake #2

Using Non-Numerical Variables

❌ Bad: "Do boys like rugby more than girls at our school?"

Why is this bad?

"Like" is categorical (yes/no), not numerical. We need something we can measure.

✓ Better: "Do boys tend to spend more hours per week playing rugby than girls at our school?"

The Question Formula

Use this template:

"Do [Group 1] tend to [verb] [more/less] [variable] than [Group 2] for [population]?"

Key phrases:

  • "tend to be..." → amounts, weights, heights
  • "tend to have..." → counts, rates, scores
  • "tend to spend..." → time or money

Why "Tend to"?

We use "tend to" because we're talking about general patterns, not every individual.

Some cats might be heavier than some dogs, but on average, dogs tend to be heavier than cats.

This acknowledges:

  • Variation exists within each group
  • We're looking for overall trends

Direction Matters!

Your question must show a direction - which group will have higher/lower values.

"Do dogs tend to be HEAVIER than cats?"

Why direction is important:

  • Shows you've thought about expectations
  • Creates a testable prediction
  • Required for NCEA assessments

From Question to Hypothesis

Turn your question into a hypothesis by making it a statement.

Comparison Question
"Do dogs tend to be heavier than cats for pets of New Zealanders?"
⬇️
Hypothesis
"Dogs tend to be heavier than cats for pets of New Zealanders."

Remember: Your hypothesis is your prediction based on research or knowledge.

Good Research Practices

Before finalizing your hypothesis, do background research:

✓ Do This:

  • Read multiple sources
  • Take notes in your own words
  • Track where information came from
  • Form your own predictions

Avoiding Plagiarism

You must avoid plagiarism! Here's what NOT to do:

❌ Don't Do This:

  • Copy text without quotation marks
  • Use someone else's hypothesis
  • Change a few words but keep structure
  • Forget to credit sources

How to Reference Research

Acknowledge your sources properly:

Example:

"Based on research from the NZ Veterinary Association (2025), I predict dogs tend to be heavier than cats for pets of New Zealanders."

Include: Source name, year, and your interpretation.

Quick Recap: The 3 Components

Every comparison question needs:

1. Two Groups
Who are you comparing?
2. Numerical Variable
What can you measure?
3. Population
Who does this apply to?

💡 Tips for Success

#
  • Always use "tend to" to acknowledge variation in data
  • Show direction: Your question must predict which group is higher/lower
  • Make sure your variable is numerical (can be measured or counted)
  • Clearly identify two distinct groups to compare
  • Define your population specifically (not just "people")
  • Use direction words: more/less, higher/lower, greater/smaller, heavier/lighter
  • Do background research to support your hypothesis, but write it in your own words
  • Always acknowledge sources if you use research to form your prediction
