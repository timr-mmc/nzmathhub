Writing Relationship Questions

#
Learning Objectives: Learn to write statistical investigative questions that explore relationships between two numerical variables. Master identifying explanatory and response variables, understanding variable types, and defining your population.

What You'll Learn

  • Understanding what makes a good relationship question
  • Identifying two numerical variables
  • Distinguishing between explanatory and response variables
  • Clearly defining the population of interest
  • Using "relationship" language appropriately

📚 Core Concepts

What is a Relationship Question?

A relationship question investigates how two numerical variables are connected.

"What is the relationship between study time and test scores for Year 11 students?"

This helps us:

  • Identify patterns and trends
  • Make predictions
  • Explore associations between variables

Bad Question vs Good Question

See the difference between poor and excellent questions:

❌ Bad Question:

"Does studying help students do better?"

Problems:

  • Variables not specified
  • No numerical measurements
  • No "relationship" language
  • Vague population

✅ Good Question:

"What is the relationship between study time (hours per week) and test scores (%) for Year 11 students?"

Why this is better:

  • ✓ Two numerical variables
  • ✓ Units specified
  • ✓ Uses "relationship" language
  • ✓ Clear population

Four Key Components

Every good relationship question needs:

1. Two Numerical Variables
Study time & Test scores
2. Numerical Variables
Both measured with numbers
3. Clear Population
Year 11 students
4. "Relationship" Language
"What is the relationship..."

Key: Both variables must be numerical!

What Are Numerical Variables?

Numerical variables are measurements that can be expressed as numbers.

Two types:

  • Discrete: Counting (specific values only)
  • Continuous: Measuring (any value in a range)
Tip: Using at least one continuous variable helps avoid stripey graphs and shows clearer patterns!

Discrete Variables

Discrete = Counting

Can only take specific values (usually whole numbers)

Examples of Discrete Variables
  • Number of siblings: 0, 1, 2, 3...
  • Goals scored: 0, 1, 2, 3...
  • Apps installed: 5, 10, 15...
  • Shoe size: 8, 9, 10...

Note: Discrete variables are numerical but limited to specific values.

Continuous Variables

Continuous = Measuring

Can take any value within a range (including decimals)

Examples of Continuous Variables
  • Height: 165.3 cm, 172.8 cm...
  • Study time: 3.5 hours, 7.25 hours...
  • Temperature: 18.6°C, 22.1°C...
  • Weight: 65.2 kg, 70.8 kg...

Key: Values can fall anywhere on a scale!

Variable Requirements

For relationship investigations, you need the right combination:

✓ Best Combinations (avoid stripey graphs):

  • Continuous + Continuous (clearest patterns)
  • Continuous + Discrete (still works well)

⚠️ Avoid if possible:

Discrete + Discrete creates "stripey" scatter plots

Explanatory Variable

The variable we think might influence or explain the other.

Explanatory Variable

Example: Study time (hours)

"We change this to see the effect"

Think: What might cause changes in the other variable?

Response Variable

The variable that responds to or is affected by the explanatory variable.

Response Variable

Example: Test scores (%)

"This might change as a result"

⚠️ Remember: Correlation ≠ Causation!

Common Mistake: Both Discrete

⚠️ Not ideal:

"What is the relationship between number of siblings and number of pets?"

Issue: Both variables are discrete (just counting) - creates stripey graphs.

✓ Better: Use at least one continuous variable

"What is the relationship between time spent with pets (hours) and test scores (%)?"

Common Mistake: Categorical Variable

❌ Bad:

"What is the relationship between favorite color and test scores?"

Why it fails: Favorite color is categorical (not numerical).

✓ Better: Both must be numerical

"What is the relationship between sleep hours and test scores (%) for Year 11 students?"

The Question Formula

Use this template:

"What is the relationship between [Variable 1 + units] and [Variable 2 + units] for [population]?"

Essential elements:

  • "What is the relationship between..."
  • Both variables with units
  • Ideally at least one continuous
  • Clear population

Specifying Units

Units make your question precise and clear.

❌ Vague
"What is the relationship between height and weight?"
⬇️
✓ Clear
"What is the relationship between height (cm) and weight (kg) for Year 11 students?"

Units help with data collection and analysis!

Defining Your Population

The population defines who or what you're studying.

✓ Good populations:

  • Year 11 students at Wellington High
  • Teenagers aged 13-19 in NZ
  • Rugby players in Auckland clubs

❌ Too vague:

  • "People" - too broad!
  • "Students" - which students?

💡 Tips for Success

#

Key Reminders:

  • Two numerical variables: Both must be measurable (not categorical)
  • Prefer continuous: At least one continuous variable avoids stripey graphs
  • Include units: Specify cm, hours, %, kg, etc.
  • Use "relationship" language: "What is the relationship between..."
  • Be specific about population: Define exactly who or what you're studying
  • Think explanatory vs response: Which variable might influence the other?
  • Remember: Correlation does not imply causation!

Quick Check - Is Your Question Good?

Ask yourself:

  1. Are both variables numerical? ✓
  2. Do I have at least one continuous variable? (Better for clear patterns) ✓
  3. Did I specify units? ✓
  4. Did I clearly define my population? ✓
  5. Does it use "relationship" language? ✓

If you answered yes to all, you're ready to investigate!
